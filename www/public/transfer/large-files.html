Why 4share Is Different

4share takes a fundamentally different approach to file transfer. Instead of uploading your files to a remote server and then giving someone a download link, 4share connects your device directly to the recipient's device. Your files travel peer to peer, which means they never sit on any server.

Because there is no server involved in storing your data, there is no storage limit to impose. You can send files of any size — 100 MB, 10 GB, 100 GB — and 4share will handle it the same way. The transfer happens directly between your browser and the recipient's browser using WebRTC, a technology built for exactly this kind of real-time data exchange.

This peer-to-peer architecture also means the transfer speed is limited only by your internet connection, not by a server's upload bandwidth. If both you and the recipient have fast connections, you get fast transfers. No throttling, no queuing, no waiting for a server to become available.

Best of all, 4share is completely free. There are no premium tiers, no bandwidth caps, no ads, and no accounts. You open the site, select your files, share the code, and the transfer happens. That is the entire process.

The recipient opens 4share.work on their device and enters the 6-digit code. The transfer begins immediately once they confirm. They do not need an account either.

Step 5: Files Transfer Directly

The files move directly from your device to the recipient's device. You can watch the progress in real time. Once the transfer is complete, the files are saved on the recipient's device. Your originals remain on your device, untouched.

Why P2P File Transfer Is Better for Large Files

Peer-to-peer file transfer has several inherent advantages when it comes to large files. The most obvious is the removal of storage limits. When you upload a file to a cloud service, you are consuming storage on their servers. Those servers cost money to operate, which is why every cloud provider imposes limits and charges for exceeding them.

With P2P transfer, there is no server storage involved. The file moves directly from your device to the recipient's device. This means there is no storage to limit, no bandwidth to throttle on the server side, and no costs to pass on to you as a user.

Speed is another advantage. When you upload a file to a cloud server, you are limited by the server's upload and download bandwidth, which is shared among all users. During peak times, transfers can slow to a crawl. With P2P transfer, the speed is determined solely by the two endpoints — your device and the recipient's device. On a fast local network, this can be dramatically faster than any cloud-based solution.

Privacy is the third major advantage. When you upload a file to a cloud service, that file is stored on their servers, often in multiple data centers for redundancy. You have no control over who has access to those servers, how long the file is retained, or what happens to it after you delete it. With P2P transfer, the file never touches a third-party server. It goes from your device to the recipient's device, and that is it.

Tips for Sending Large Files Efficiently

Here are some practical tips to make your large file transfers as smooth as possible:

Use a wired connection when possible. While WiFi works fine for most transfers, a wired Ethernet connection provides more consistent speeds for very large files. If you are sending a 50 GB file and have the option to plug in, do it.

Keep both devices online. Since 4share transfers files directly between devices, both the sender and receiver need to be connected to the internet throughout the transfer. If either device goes offline, the transfer pauses and resumes when the connection is restored.

Share the code quickly. The 6-digit code is your transfer key. Share it with the recipient as soon as it is generated so they can start the download while your device is still online and ready.

Close unnecessary applications. Other applications that use your internet connection can compete for bandwidth. Closing them during a large transfer can improve speed.

Check available disk space. Make sure the recipient's device has enough free disk space for the incoming files. This sounds obvious, but it is easy to overlook when sending a large batch of files.
