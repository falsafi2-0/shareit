Server-based file transfer services store your files on their servers during the transfer process. While convenient, this creates multiple security risks that most users never consider. Here's why server-based file transfer is fundamentally risky.

What Is Server-Based File Transfer?

Services like WeTransfer, Send-Anywhere, and Google Drive all follow the same pattern:

  1. Sender uploads files to the company's servers
  2. Files sit on those servers until the recipient downloads them
  3. After download (or timeout), files are deleted from the servers

During steps 1-3, your files exist on infrastructure you don't control, managed by a company you may not trust.

Risk 1: Data Breaches

Every server is a target. Companies storing your files must defend against hackers 24/7. A single vulnerability means your files could be exposed.

Cloud storage breaches exposed billions of files in recent years. When a service stores millions of files, it becomes a high-value target.

Risk 2: Unauthorized Access

Employees at the file transfer company can potentially access your files. While most reputable companies restrict access, the technical possibility exists. You're trusting:

  • System administrators with root access
  • Support staff who may access files during troubleshooting
  • Third-party contractors managing infrastructure
  • Any new employee hired in the future

Risk 3: Data Retention

How long do servers actually keep your files? The privacy policy says one thing, but the reality can be different:

  • Deleted files may persist in backups for months
  • Server drives may be improperly decommissioned
  • Metadata (who sent what to whom, when) is often retained longer
  • Some services have been caught retaining "deleted" files

Risk 4: Government Requests

File transfer companies receive government data requests. Under laws like the CLOUD Act, US-based companies can be compelled to hand over your data — even if it's stored overseas.

Your files may be accessible to:

  • Law enforcement with a warrant
  • Intelligence agencies with national security letters
  • Foreign governments through mutual legal assistance treaties

Risk 5: Third-Party Sharing

Many file transfer services use third-party infrastructure:

  • Cloud hosting providers (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure)
  • CDN providers for faster delivery
  • Analytics services that log metadata
  • Advertising networks that track user behavior

Your files may touch more servers than you realize.

Risk 6: Server Downtime

When servers go down, your files are inaccessible. Service outages can last hours or days. If you're relying on a file being available, server dependency creates a single point of failure.

The P2P Alternative

Peer-to-peer file transfer eliminates all server-based risks:

  • No server storage — files never leave your device until they reach the recipient
  • End-to-end encryption — WebRTC encrypts all data in transit
  • No data retention — no company stores your files or metadata
  • No government requests — there's nothing to hand over
  • No single point of failure — direct device-to-device connection

Frequently Asked Questions

Are all server-based services risky?
Yes, to varying degrees. Any service that stores your files on their servers introduces the risks discussed. The risk level depends on the company's security practices, but the fundamental architecture creates exposure.
How does P2P avoid these risks?
P2P transfers files directly between devices. There's no server to breach, no data to retain, and no company to serve subpoenas to. The only intermediary is a signaling server that handles connection setup, not file data.
Is P2P transfer completely risk-free?
No system is 100% risk-free. P2P still has risks like man-in-the-middle attacks (mitigated by WebRTC encryption) and endpoint security. But it eliminates the major server-side risks.
